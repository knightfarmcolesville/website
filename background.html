Historical Background

The Knight Family Farm and the Josiah Stowell Home — where the first branch of the Church began

The Joseph Knight Sr. Farm and the Knight Family

A Prosperous Farm in the Susquehanna Valley

Joseph Knight Sr. was a prosperous farmer, miller, and grain dealer who purchased farmland on the east side of the Susquehanna River in what is now Nineveh, New York, around 1810. The property included a working farm, a carding mill, and a grist mill. It was here, in 1825, that Knight hired a young Joseph Smith as a farmhand — a decision that would place the Knight family at the very center of the unfolding Restoration.

“The Second Family of the Restoration”

Knight quickly recognized the character of his young employee. He later recalled that Joseph Smith was “the best hand he ever hired.” As their friendship deepened, Smith confided in Knight about the angelic visitations he had received and the sacred record he had been told about. Knight believed him. This made the Knights among the very earliest believers in Joseph Smith’s prophetic calling, accepting his testimony even before Oliver Cowdery, Martin Harris, or David Whitmer entered the picture. For this reason, historians have called the Knight family “the Second Family of the Restoration,” second only to the Smith family itself.

Material Support for the Work of the Restoration

The Knights’ support went far beyond belief. Joseph Knight Sr. provided critical material assistance during some of the most pivotal moments of the Restoration. He lent Joseph Smith his horse and wagon for trips to court Emma Hale. On the night of September 22, 1827, Knight and his friend Josiah Stowell were present at the Smith family home, waiting anxiously as Joseph Smith went to the Hill Cumorah to receive the golden plates from the angel Moroni. Knight later provided the wagon Joseph used to transport the plates.

During the translation of the Book of Mormon in 1828 and 1829, Knight made multiple trips to deliver food, paper, and other supplies to sustain Joseph and his scribes as they worked. Without the Knights’ generosity, the translation effort would have faced far greater hardship. In recognition of his faithfulness, the Lord gave a revelation specifically for Joseph Knight Sr., now recorded as Doctrine and Covenants Section 12, encouraging him to assist in bringing forth the work of God.

The First Miracle and the Colesville Baptisms

The Knight farm was the site of some of the earliest and most dramatic events in church history. In April 1830, shortly after the Church was organized on April 6, Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery traveled to Colesville to preach. At a meeting in the Knight home, Newel Knight, Joseph Sr.’s son, was afflicted by an evil spirit. The Prophet Joseph Smith exercised his priesthood authority and cast the spirit out. Newel Knight later testified that he saw the spirit leave his body and that he was immediately filled with peace. This event is recognized as the first miracle performed in the Church after its organization.

The farm also witnessed the faith and courage required of early converts. In late June 1830, Joseph Smith returned to Colesville to baptize the growing number of believers. The Saints dammed a small stream on the property to create a pool suitable for baptism. That night, a mob tore down the dam. Undeterred, the Saints rebuilt it early the next morning, and on June 28, 1830, thirteen people were baptized, including Emma Smith, Joseph Knight Sr.’s wife Polly Peck Knight, and several other members of the extended Knight family.

Before the newly baptized members could even be confirmed, a mob of about fifty people surrounded the Knight home, and Joseph Smith was arrested on false charges of being “a disorderly person, of setting the country in an uproar by preaching the Book of Mormon.” Knight hired two able attorneys, James Davidson and John Reid, to defend the Prophet. Joseph was acquitted after trial, only to be immediately arrested again on similar charges in a neighboring county. He was acquitted a second time.

The Colesville Branch — The First Branch of the Church

These conversions formed the nucleus of the Colesville Branch, widely regarded as the first branch of the Church. The branch was formally organized in the fall of 1830, with roughly sixty to eighty members drawn from an extended network of interconnected families — the Knights, Pecks, DeMilles, Stringhams, Culvers, Slades, and others. Hyrum Smith initially served as branch president, followed by Newel Knight.

What makes the Colesville Branch remarkable is not only its early formation but its extraordinary unity and faithfulness. When the Saints were commanded to gather to Ohio in 1831, the entire branch migrated together, traveling as a body. When the Ohio settlement fell through after a landowner retracted his offer of property, they pressed on to Jackson County, Missouri, arriving on July 26, 1831 — becoming the first branch of the Church to settle the land that had been dedicated as Zion.

Polly Knight, Joseph Sr.’s beloved wife, was gravely ill during the journey but insisted on pressing forward, saying she wanted to set her feet on the land of Zion. She died shortly after arriving, becoming the first Church member to pass away in that land. The Colesville Saints continued to move with the body of the Church through Missouri, to Illinois, and eventually westward. Joseph Knight Sr. himself died in 1847 at the Winter Quarters encampment at Mt. Pisgah, Iowa, faithful to the end.

Preservation of the Farm

The Knight farmhouse, believed to date to approximately 1815, stood for nearly two centuries but fell into disrepair. Beginning around 2004, three families — Raphael and Shari Mecham, Steve and Pat Glenn, and Paul and Ann Painter — privately purchased and undertook an extensive restoration of the property. After nearly thirteen years of painstaking work and the help of many volunteers from across the country, the Joseph Knight Sr. Ancestral Farmhouse opened to visitors in the summer of 2016.

The home is not owned or operated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. It is maintained privately and is open for guided tours during summer months. The farmhouse features period furnishings and artifacts and serves as a place where visitors can connect with the faith, sacrifice, and fellowship that characterized the earliest days of the Restoration.

The Josiah Stowell Home

A Respected Member of the Community

Josiah Stowell Sr. was born on March 22, 1770, in Winchester, New Hampshire. By 1791 he had settled in the township of Jericho (later renamed Bainbridge, and today the village of Afton) in Chenango County, New York, where he became a respected farmer, sawmill owner, and member of the local Presbyterian congregation. He married Miriam Bridgeman and raised a large family. He was a man of means and standing in his community.

Hiring Joseph Smith — A Providential Meeting

In October 1825, Stowell traveled to the Palmyra area and hired young Joseph Smith to assist with a project near Harmony, Pennsylvania, where Stowell believed a lost Spanish silver mine could be found. While the mining venture was ultimately abandoned, this employment proved providential in two important ways. First, it brought Joseph Smith to the Harmony area, where he met and fell in love with Emma Hale, the woman who would become his wife. Second, it forged a lasting bond between Stowell and Smith, one built on Stowell’s growing conviction that the young man was called of God.

An Early and Steadfast Supporter

Stowell became one of Joseph Smith’s earliest and most steadfast supporters. When charges were brought against Joseph Smith in 1826, accusing him of disorderly conduct, Stowell appeared in court and testified firmly in the Prophet’s defense. According to one account of the proceedings, Stowell declared that he did not merely believe in Joseph’s abilities — he said he “positively know it to be true.” Joseph was not convicted.

On the night of September 22, 1827, Josiah Stowell, along with Joseph Knight Sr., was present at the Smith family home in Manchester, New York, when Joseph Smith went to the Hill Cumorah and received the golden plates. Stowell was among the very first people to learn directly from Joseph about the sacred record. He assisted Joseph and Emma after their marriage in January 1827, and may have hosted the newlywed couple at his home on the evening of their wedding in South Bainbridge.

Faithful to the End

When the Church was organized in 1830, Stowell was among the early converts baptized in the Colesville-Bainbridge area. He stood by Joseph Smith during the Prophet’s 1830 arrest and trial in the region, once again bearing witness in his defense. Stowell remained faithful to his testimony of the Restoration for the rest of his life. He died on May 12, 1844, in Smithboro, Tioga County, New York, just weeks before the martyrdom of Joseph Smith.

The Home Today

The Josiah Stowell home, located at 323 State Highway 7 in Afton, New York, has been preserved and restored alongside the nearby Knight farm. Like the Knight property, it is not owned or operated by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints but is maintained privately and available for guided tours. The home stands as a testament to the quiet, steady faith of a man who recognized truth early and never wavered from it. Stowell’s willingness to hire, defend, and sustain Joseph Smith at critical moments helped make possible some of the most important events of the Restoration.
